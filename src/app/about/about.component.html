What is a cooperative?

A cooperative (or co-op) is a democratic business owned and run by the people who work for the business. They are the shareholders, they elect their own managers and directors, in other words, they can hire and fire their own bosses. They also decide what to do with any surplus value or profits, how much to reinvest and how much to pay to themselves. In some cases, they don't even elect leaders and all decisions are made with direct democracy, all members can be involved in making all decisions. This is usually only practical in smaller co-ops with less than 50 members


The benefits of democratic business

There are numerous benefits to workplace democracy. Worker cooperatives tend to have a longer lifespan than capitalist businesses. In medium to large sized co-ops the workers are usually better paid than workers for capitalist firms. Their jobs are usually much more secure, a cooperative is much less likely to fire workers. Democratic businesses are usually more productive, efficient and give better value for money. The ratio between the highest and lowest rates of pay tends to be much lower, around 4:1 on average. The members work with much more enthusiasm because they work for themselves and are not being exploited by shareholder or bossed around and bullied by corporate executives.

Why the labour theory of value is important

Of course, cooperation and democratic decision making can lead to smarter, better informed group choices but if we want a genuinely fair economy with as little exploitation as possible it should be accompanied by an understanding and implemenentation of the labour theory of value first popularised by the classical economists David Ricardo and Adam Smith. The basic idea is that the value of all goods and services is roughly proportional to the human labour time invested in creating them, with some variation depending on the supply and demand. All the accounts gathered by the statistics bureaus of all developed countries show a very strong correlation between value created and human labour time, roughly 95%. When we take goods off the shelf, it is usually very difficult/impossible to know exactly how much labour time has gone into creating them because this information is not shared between the companies involved or the shop that sells the product. However, government statistics bureaus do gather and publish data about the total number of hours worked in each industry and the total value created in each industry. This can be plotted on a graph, and there is almost universally a very strong correlation. We can also calculate the average amount of value created per hour of human labour simply by adding all the value created in all industries then dividing it by the sum of all hours worked in all industries. In Australia in 2020, the average value created per hour of labour was $83 AUD, the average wage was only $59. The median hourly rate of pay was only $36. Even without factoring in the obscenely high cost of living, Australians already have between 71 and 43% of the value they create for society stolen from them by shareholders. More than 90% of people would benefit financially from a socialist economic system grounded in the labour theory of value.
